## Outbound: US → Paris → Cahors → Festival

### Flights
- Fly into **Paris CDG** (cheapest transatlantic hub)
- Take **Intercités** Paris Gare d'Austerlitz → Cahors (~5.5h, ~€20–25)
  - **Reserve a bike space at the same time** (€10/bike, mandatory May–Sep, limited spaces — book early)

> **Why not Bordeaux?** Bordeaux is 320km *west* of the festival. Going there first means a 3h train backtrack before you even start biking. Skip it entirely. Cahors is 55km from the festival — direct.

### Cahors → Saint-Jean-de-Laur (~55 km)
- **Route:** V86 Véloroute Vallée du Lot — signed, paved, follows the Lot River gorge
- **Surface:** ✅ Paved roads, manageable on 25mm tires (short unpaved links easily bypassed)
- **V86 status:** Actively expanding — new sections opened 2024 between Cahors–Arcambal and Cajarc–Larroque-Toirac. Check current conditions at [cahorsvalleedulot.com](https://www.cahorsvalleedulot.com/explorer/evadez-vous-sur-les-chemins/la-veloroute-de-la-vallee-du-lot/) before departure.
- Cahors is a stunning medieval city — worth spending a night before biking to the festival

## Return: Festival → Cahors → Train → Angoulême → Paris

### ⚠️ Heat Advisory
July in the Lot and Charente can hit 35°C+. **Start riding by 7am, stop 12–3pm in shade, resume late afternoon.** Carry minimum 2 litres between stops. The Angoulême → Poitiers section crosses open plateau with no shade and potential southwest headwinds — stock up in Angoulême.

### Single Speed Notes
- The terrain between Cahors and the Dordogne (Sarlat direction) crosses the **limestone causse** — sustained climbing that's genuinely brutal on a single speed. Skip it by train.
- Riding out of **Angoulême** involves a real climb (the city sits on a promontory). Head south via Soyaux/La Couronne for the flattest exit. You'll feel this first thing in the morning.
- **Gear ratio:** 42×16 or 42×17 is ideal for this trip. Don't go higher than 42×16 or you'll grind on the Lot valley ramps. The Loire section is flat enough that you'll spin comfortably all day.

### Your Girlfriend Meets You in Angoulême

**After festival (July 5):** bike 55km back to Cahors, then train to Angoulême via Agen and Bordeaux (~4.5h, one or two changes, ~€30–40 + bike). Aim to arrive Angoulême **July 6 evening**.

She takes **TGV Paris Montparnasse → Angoulême** (~2h10, ~€30–60 booked ahead). Angoulême is on the main Paris–Bordeaux TGV line — easiest connection possible.

**Why Angoulême:**
- Puts you right at the start of the flat Loire corridor
- She doesn't need to fly anywhere — just TGV from Paris
- Angoulême is a beautiful hilltop city with a world-famous **Cité Internationale de la Bande Dessinée** (comics/graphic novel museum) — worth a full evening
- Skips all the hilly causse and Dordogne terrain

### Route Notes
- **Tours → Orléans:** The signed Loire à Vélo uses gravel riverbank paths — ignore those entirely. Ride **D952 Tours → Amboise → Blois**, then **D951 Blois → Beaugency → Orléans**. Both are paved departmental roads that hug the Loire the whole way. Scenic, light traffic, fully paved.
- **Orléans → Paris by train:** Don't bike the last stretch. **Orléans → Paris Austerlitz is ~1h on TER** (~€15–20 + bike). Bikes go in the luggage van — reserve at sncf-connect.com. This is cleaner than fighting your way through banlieue traffic.
- Accueil Vélo hosts (700+ certified) are searchable at francevelotourisme.com — they're within 5 km of the road route, not just the riverbank path.
- **GPX:** Build on Komoot routing to D952/D951 directly, not the auto-suggested "Loire à Vélo" option.

Pack 2 spare inner tubes + patch kit.

## Highlights & Things to See

### Lot Valley (Cahors → Festival and back)

**Pont Valentré, Cahors** — UNESCO World Heritage medieval fortified bridge with three towers. It's 2 minutes from the city center. Walk across it the evening you arrive.

**Vers canyon** (~15km from Cahors) — The Lot gorge narrows dramatically. The cliffs close in on both sides.

**Bouzies** (~30km from Cahors) — Look for the *chemin de halage* (towpath) carved directly into the cliff face. It was cut for horses towing barges in the 19th century. Looks physically impossible. You can walk a section of it.

**Saint-Cirq-Lapopie** (~25km from Cahors) ⭐ — This is non-negotiable. One of the most beautiful villages in France: a cluster of medieval houses perched on a 100m limestone cliff directly above the Lot river. André Breton called it "the impossible rose." There's a short steep climb off the river road to reach it. Budget 45–60 minutes. Do not skip this.

**Figeac** (~25km from the festival) — Birthplace of Jean-François Champollion, who decoded the Rosetta Stone. The Musée Champollion is excellent, and there's a giant replica of the Rosetta Stone embedded in the town square outside. Beautiful medieval center.

### Loire Valley (Tours → Orléans, D952/D951)

**Amboise** (~25km from Tours) ⭐ — Two things here:
1. **Château d'Amboise**: French royal residence, atmospheric, sits directly above the town.
2. **Clos Lucé**: Leonardo da Vinci spent the final three years of his life here as a personal guest of François I and died in this house in 1519. The gardens have full-scale bronze models of his inventions (flying machines, armored car, swing bridge). Budget 1.5–2 hours.

**Chaumont-sur-Loire** (~42km from Tours) ⭐ — The **Festival International des Jardins** runs **April 22–November 1, 2026** — it's on while you ride through. Thirty international garden designers take over the château grounds with site-specific installations. Genuinely strange and beautiful. Right on the D952. Plan a stop: [domaine-chaumont.fr](https://domaine-chaumont.fr/)

**Chambord** (~15km detour from Blois) — François I's hunting lodge, the largest Loire château. The double-helix staircase (where two people can ascend and descend simultaneously without seeing each other) is allegedly designed with Leonardo's involvement. The scale of the building is absurd — 440 rooms, 365 fireplaces. If you're going to do one detour off the main road, this is it.

**Blois** — The Château Royal de Blois contains four distinct architectural wings from four different centuries, all in one courtyard. The wing built for Louis XII has the famous *cabinet rooms* of Catherine de Médicis — small hidden chambers in the walls allegedly used to store poisons and state documents.

**Beaugency** (~85km from Tours, between Blois and Orléans) — Quick stop. The Tour de César (a massive Romanesque keep) just stands in the town square with no barrier around it, predating most of the Loire châteaux by 200 years.

**Orléans** — The Cathédrale Sainte-Croix has one of the finest Gothic naves in France. The stained glass windows tell the entire story of Joan of Arc in sequence around the nave.

## Water & Café Stops

### Lot Valley V86 (Cahors → Festival / Festival → Cahors)
- **Cahors** — full city, stock up here
- **Vers** (~15 km east of Cahors) — water fountain, small village
- **Saint-Cirq-Lapopie** (~25 km) — café and ice cream at the top. Worth the climb.
- **Cajarc** (~40 km from Cahors) — café, boulangerie, last real stop
- Between Cajarc and the festival (15 km): no shops — top up in Cajarc

### Angoulême → Tours (D-roads)
- **Ruffec** (~45 km from Angoulême) — small town, boulangerie, café
- **Châtellerault** (~75 km from Poitiers) — good lunch town before Tours
- Villages every 15–20 km, water fountain in each square

### Tours → Orléans (D952 / D951, Loire Valley)
- **Amboise** (~25 km from Tours) — Clos Lucé, boulangerie, café, camping. Full stop.
- **Chaumont-sur-Loire** (~17 km from Amboise) — château, gardens festival, café
- **Blois** (~55 km from Tours) — major town, everything available
- **Beaugency** (between Blois and Orléans) — medieval tower, café
- **Orléans** — full city, last stop before the train home

### Orléans → Paris (by train, not bike)
Take the TER train — ~1h, bikes in luggage van. Reserve at sncf-connect.com.

### General Tips
- French **boulangeries** open 7–7:30 AM, closed Sunday afternoon and one weekday
- **Tabacs** (tobacco shops) also sell snacks, maps, phone credit
- **Fountains** (*fontaines d'eau potable*) in every village square — drink freely
- **Supermarkets:** Intermarché and Leclerc Drives often on town outskirts — good for bulk resupply
- Ask for "une carafe d'eau" at any café — free tap water, always
